Clarity and transparency

  • Always prioritise ensuring the user’s expectations and the capability of the feature are aligned
    • Clearly describe what the feature does and what it does not enable
    • Ensure your feature and content does not create new unmet expectations for related features

      Example observed in research
      An example of this that we observed in user research was around personalised and curated content.

      When users saw information that had been curated for them on an ‘eligibility page’ they assumed it was accurate and that the amounts cited were the specific amounts they would receive as part of a benefit claim.
  • Ensure the user can see why something is happening (for example, be specific about how a page is being personalised, or why a service is being suggested)
  • Be clear about impact to the user - some features could lead to both user-perceived positive and negative outcomes (for example, a new entitlement versus a benefit being turned off)
  • Ensure the user can review what has happened and when
  • Be clear about responsibilities - as the account becomes feature rich, it may allude to a shift in responsibilities away from the user

Control and consent

  • Ensure the feature gives the user control and seeks their permission
  • Most of all, users need to retain control of:
    • Their data - nothing should go in or out of the account without permission and notification
    • The use of data - all features must be user controllable
    • Who gets to see their data - for example, for the information passed to services, users must always control the final ‘GO’ step

UI scalability

  • Consider how a feature will scale when the account (and data within it) gets very large
    • How will the UI tolerate this?
    • Will the UI still be clear and simple to use?
  • Be aware of tipping points when using the UI could become more overhead than the efficiency and/or accuracy goals they are supporting

Understand the needs of citizen users

  • As described in our approach to user needs, our user needs have been collated as an output of our rounds of research with Universal Credit claimants. We recommend continued learning in relation to other types of service users and their needs
  • Understand how GOV.UK account features would impact user needs, either positively or adversely.
  • Employ tech solutions which truly meet user needs.

Continue user research to understand how users might interact with features in order to serve their personal preferences and needs

Understand how users would interact with a feature through observation and discussion.

An example of this is our work around a place to upload documents.

User research participants envisaged that they may use this as a space to upload previous address documents, letters with reference numbers, etc.

Consider how the feature should be best used and designed with clarity.

Continue usability testing

Continue usability testing as the programme of work progresses. The designs we have used as part of our work are primarily for concept testing.

Create a secure service which protects users’ privacy

Concerns around privacy and security was a clear theme coming through in our user insights. In direct echo of service standard 9 - provide a secure service.

Listen to the way users describe things in their own words

Users’ natural language and their descriptions of the GOV.UK account may help with profile, branding, and content.

Operating model

  • The operating model needs to be established and functional to support the roll out of the features.
  • Clearly define the operating model support mechanism.
  • Clearly communicate to the end user and service teams which contact centre users need to engage with to discuss their GOV.UK account, the data and functionality within it.

Educating services

  • Consider how to educate government services about how the GOV.UK account is going to change.
    • Senior leadership teams will be investing personal capital into the programme and need to have visibility and confidence in the changes happening.
    • As features are added to the GOV.UK account this will fundamentally impact service roadmaps and delivery priorities.

Data maturity

Some very sensitive types of data will need to be excluded (for example, special forces personnel).

  • Under GDPR users should only provide information that is necessary, but many of the cross government features will give greater value as more user data is available. A balance will need to be reached between minimising use of data and maximising benefit from account features.
  • Some data will only ever be service specific - storing and sharing data with high reuse potential should be prioritised.
  • Development, implementation and maintenance of a cross government data dictionary, or ongoing cross government data matching need to be considered.
  • Recognition that different services will require different levels of data verification. Data standards vary across different services - services will need to understand the verification standard associated with each data item so it can decide how it can use the data.

Joining up systems and back office processes

  • Understand how the GOV.UK account will onboard services with a heavy reliance on manual processes or low digital maturity. Many services are heavily reliant on manual back office processes to support their digital services.
  • Awareness that government services are transforming at different rates.
  • Understand how backend systems and processes will work together with the GOV.UK account.
  • For services looking to procure new backend systems, support to procure and implement systems that will enable information sharing with the GOV.UK account.
