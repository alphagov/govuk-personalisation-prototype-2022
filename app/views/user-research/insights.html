Generally speaking, users reacted positively to the concept of the GOV.UK account as part of a x-gov transaction, however, there were various comments around its usefulness being dependent on whether or not there was a genuine reason to use it.

Comments in user research highlighted the fact that throughout certain periods of life (i.e. childcare) the likelihood of interacting with government services is much higher.

For some, depending on lifestyle, the regularity of interactions with the government is much lower. Some users clearly articulated that they would find the account useful ‘now’ (during childcare) whereas otherwise they may not see the point.

Further work around profile raising and adoption of the account will likely be needed in the future to understand how best to compel users to create an account and engage with its features.

There will be some data sceptics within the future audience who will be unlikely to save data to a GOV.UK account. This project does not seek to quantify or predict this potential segment.

Users also expected that if they entered data to a GOV.UK account, this data would be shared across all government services. This upholds findings from previous rounds of user research carried out by GOV.UK teams.

As a team, we refer to this idea of data sharing as ‘global push’. That is, if a user adds, amends or deletes data, it will automatically be updated, or ‘pushed’, across the account and relevant government services.

The expectation that ‘global push’ occurs, was a recurring theme throughout our user research sessions, and one that presents a risk in terms of human impact due to the gap between that expectation and reality.

If users engage in a transaction and believe that the data has been pushed and processed by all relevant services, when in reality that is not the case, the outcome could be detrimental - particularly in scenarios relating to benefits and eligibility.

Not all users will want to save everything to their GOV.UK account.

Users said that there were some data items they would not save - for example, details subject to change such as weekly pay, and their children’s personal data.

As part of a cross-team exercise, we asked users about their perspective on data storage.

As expected, the average citizen will not be able to articulate preferences around storage. They do, however, reference security.

The reason for posing this question in research, is that there has been some discussion in the programme about the use of solid PODS.

Whilst this work is now being owned by another team, we recommended that the following end user issues be considered:

  1. Whether control and ownership over data is a positive thing for citizens - we recommend an impact assessment to better understand worst case scenarios. Whilst solid PODS would give users the choice over whether they own their data, we would argue that choice is not always a good thing.
  2. Whether PODS would entail any transfer of responsibility to users, and if so, what risks and concerns could that cause for citizen users. Can they realistically comprehend the complexities and consequences?
  3. Would any future transfer of responsibility go against the intrinsic user need of ‘reducing cognitive load’?
  4. Do PODS meet user needs?

The theme of security was mentioned by users throughout the rounds of user research.

We explored concepts which could be introduced in the future as a move to a more ‘proactive’ government.

In phase 2, round 2, we tested the concept of an automated recurring transaction relating to childcare, as well as the ability to {{ data['features']['30'] }} to the childcare provider.

Automation in this way received conflicting reviews. Some users saw this as a useful, more accurate way of processing a transaction, however, others were concerned that an error in automation may result in an inaccurate transaction which may lead to an issue with a benefit payment.

A note on our team experience of user research is relevant here - the participants we spoke to in round 2 served as an important reminder that we work with people with complex lives.

Although automation may be part of future roadmap or strategy, it should be carefully managed so that users do not incur errors which could impact their lives.
