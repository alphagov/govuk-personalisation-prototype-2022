Most of the services included within the research told us that they are reliant on manual processes to support their online service.

The service and data research also highlighted the use data from 3rd parties, (e.g. Ordnance Survey data, 3rd party address lookups and use of Experian data).

Services had various assumptions about how the GOV.UK account could benefit them.

The benefits of joining up services were often specific to the service and the needs of their end users - these additional service-centric benefits covered in detail in each of the case studies.

However, across the sessions, services shared the expectation that they could benefit from from data sharing within the GOV account in the following ways:

  • Help enable services to keep accurate records
    • Reduce fraud and misuse of the service
    • Improve communication by only sending out relevant information
  • Empowers services to exchange data freely
  • Reduce manual processing
  • Cost savings (e.g. use of 3rd party data, development costs, staff costs)

5.1 Differences in data assurance standards across government could make consuming data from some government services difficult.

  • Some services may only be willing to be a source of data in the future, rather than a consumer of data, and may not see the benefits of participating in data sharing activity within the context of a GOV.UK account.
  • Despite hesitation from some services, there was also acknowledgement that different services require different levels of adherence to standards “why would a government department move up to the highest standard because that’s going to have an impact on the usability of their service?”
{{ data['example'] }}

Identity Assurance level 4/3 - the highest levels - most other departments aren't there - the stakes are high - I can't see how we leverage the trust with other departments.

5.2 Current legislation and data sharing policy were also seen as significant future challenges.

In particular:


  • The time it takes to get agreements in place stifles innovation: It currently takes 18 months - 2 years
  • Services spoke about individual consent: “user needs and user rights”
  • Changes are needed: Some services believe that without changes to policy and legislation, this future work to join up government services may not be possible.
{{ data['example'] }}

I worry about all the data sharing agreements, taking 18 months for it to go through. Can we get a DSA done in time that won't stifle innovations for 12, 24 months.

Service users are unclear in their expectations of the future operating model.

Many speculated about what would happen if the data is wrong somewhere, and the user needs to change it.

  • What is the support mechanism?
  • If the user contacts the contact centre - who's contact centre is this?
  • Will the GOV.UK GDS contact centre need to resolve this or the service the data has come from?
  • How will this be explained to the user?
! Warning Services expect that the operating model would be established, functional and well understood before features are implemented

Some of the services felt that a number of the proposed features would need to be carefully considered from a user engagement perspective.

Getting users to engage with their GOV.UK account to manage their data and account settings was seen by many as a difficult challenge to overcome in the future - in particular with features such as profile completion, notifications and reminders.

Services felt that it would be important to explore how to strike a healthy balance between the government knowing a great deal about the user while also helping them.

{% endblock %} {% else %} {% endif %} {% if pagemenu[11] %} {% block section12 %}
  • Some data will only ever be service specific - some services speculated about how useful it would be to save some types of data within the account if it’s unlikely to be reused. They also raised that it could become unmanageable if large amounts of data are saved to the account.
  • Some types of data will need to be excluded - there will be exceptions to data sharing (e.g. special forces personnel) where the data is too sensitive to share across government services.
  • GDPR - Users should only provide information that is necessary - there is a risk of asking for data without an explicit need - such as completing your profile
  • Services had concerns about the risk of making too many assumptions based on data - such as derived data
