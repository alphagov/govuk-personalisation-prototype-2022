In the second phase of the project, we wanted to broaden our research to include government services.

Our main focus was to explore the feasibility of joining up services across government, whilst identifying what service features would help make the GOV.UK account more personalised.

For the research, we wanted to represent the needs and views of a diverse range of government service teams.

We did this by broadening the recruitment profile beyond the services within the life event (‘Life while having a baby’) to include a range of central government services and small local authorities.

Objectives

  1. Determine the complexity vs. value of different account features identified in the backlog
  2. Capture the risks, dependencies and complexities of joining up services

Assumptions

We framed the sessions based on the following:

  • Looking 5-10 years into the future
  • All services use the GOV.UK account single sign-on
  • The account has the capability to save and reuse data
  • There are a number of account features that potentially could be available to users to create a personalised experience

What we wanted to find out

  • Understand service expectations and challenges for each feature
  • Explore how each of the features could deliver future value to a service
  • Explore the perceived complexity of adopting each feature
    • Any risks services associate with the feature
    • Possible impact on the service
    • Considerations around design/technical/legal/behavioural/cultural
    • Any other possible consequences of the feature

Methodology

We conducted an interview and moderated card sorting with service owners and product managers.

Any features associated with account setup were excluded from the initial service research. We focused on prioritising features that could be enabled after the account has the capability to save and reuse data.

We discussed each of these 13 hypothetical account features and asked participants to card sort each feature in 2 ways:

  • the perceived potential future value to the service
  • the perceived complexity of adoption

Challenges

Due to limited availability at the end of the year, many service teams were unable to support with user research.

We were unable to recruit services that exactly matched those within our life event (‘Life when having a baby’).

To overcome this, we recruited a wide range of services users (across local and central government) to make sure our findings are representative of service needs.

Research limitations

The methodology we used allowed us to explore features with service teams, learning where they saw value and complexity.

It’s important to remember that the final rankings are opinions and expectations based on a short description of each feature.

Much more detailed research is needed to fully understand the true value and complexity of adopting each of the features.

These sessions were conducted with service owners and product managers and the prioritisation exercise was not based on any technical assessments of complexity.
