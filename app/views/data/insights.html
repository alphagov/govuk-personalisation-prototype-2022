To fully support the features we’ve been testing with users and services in phase 2, services need the following capabilities in place:

  • To have a digital front end path for customers
  • To seamlessly load service application data into their backend systems
  • To share information digitally with their users
  • For this information to be stored in structured and consistent formats

We engaged with services to understand their data and process gaps against these capabilities.

1. Paper to digital frontend

Many services are trying to move away from their current paper applications, which must be manually input into systems and may not be digitised at all.

For Child Benefit, the HMRC Ops team receive 700,000 paper applications per year.

Unique information needs

Services may need situation-specific information, for example something relating to a health condition that can’t be linked to an account attribute.

{{ data['example'] }}

MOD War Pension and Armed Forces Compensation Scheme caseworkers must collect information about the incident for which the applicant is claiming compensation, including medical documentation of the event and any treatment sought.

The exact information required depends on the individual application and may not be linked to an attribute like a diagnosis, particularly as the incident may pre-date digitised NHS records.


Consent to receive information

Where services need to gather situation-specific information, they require a digital method to prove the user has consented and that is accessible to and recognised by staff in the requesting services.

{{ data['example'] }}

In the MOD War Pension and Armed Forces Compensation Scheme, where caseworkers need to request information from the NHS, they must show the user has consented to have their medical records shared.

As with the point above, this consent is specific to the information required.


Notifying non-account holders

Service journeys may have people involved who aren’t involved in the application but who need to receive information.

{{ data['example'] }}

In the Lasting Power of Attorney service, applicants may include the details of People to be notified, who are not otherwise involved in the process but may register an objection if they have concerns about how the LPA will be used.

People involved in this way may not have a GOV.UK account, or it may not be linked to the application, requiring the service to maintain alternative ways of contacting them, e.g. post.

What are the most frequently asked for data items?

Item name Frequency
Postcode 19

Unsurprisingly, the most commonly asked for data items were all personal details, required by over 50% of the 28 most visited services.

It wasn’t possible to tell if Date of birth was required for identity verification or if the services need the user's age for service provision.

To what extent do these frequently visited services ask for the same information?

The 28 services plotted against how many of the 6 common data items they require


The graph shows two main groups:

  • Highly user-centred services
    14 services ask for all 6 data items.
    Services include, Book a theory test and Register to vote.
    These services would maximise benefit from features that reuse users data.
  • Non user-centred services
    6 services use none of the 6 data items.
    These include Drive in a clean air zone and Check someone’s driving licence information.
    These services are less likely to benefit, but may ask for other data items not covered by this analysis.

How do these compare to other services?

The number of data items collected by the most visited 28 services, compared to all services


Approximately 80 different data items are collected across all 376 services, and the top 28 services collect 47 different data items.

Over 90% of services ask for less than 20 data items, and over 80% ask for 1-20 data items, which excludes niche services, e.g. Flood warning, that do not collect data in their service journey.

Data sources

Two data sources were used:

  • List of data in Central Government
    Survey results on the data items each service collects (376 services, 80 different data items)

  • GOV.UK Content Data
    Transaction pages activity data (365 services)

The analysis took the 50 most frequently visited service pages and matched them against survey responses.

Variations in service name and URL limited data matching, resulting in 28 identified services.
